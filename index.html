Personal statement

My scientific journey thus far has purposefully been an interdisciplinary one. Early in my academic career, I branched out from my major of study to learn the fundamentals of engineering design. Soon, I was leading a rover suspension team for a NASA-hosted competition team and even worked as an R&D intern at an engineering firm specializing in safety hardware. I soon transitioned back to wet-lab biochemistry where I have research experience characterizing and synthesizing novel materials and compounds. It was during these projects, that I realized that most problems in the world can be solved with better data and, more importantly, better interpretations of that data.

So I started building data analysis workflows myself. I developed machine learning pipelines for dynamic light scattering data from DNA nanoparticles, taught myself neural network and deep learning fundamentals, and began thinking about the biological systems underlying what I was studying, similar to how a systems engineer would think about a vehicle: interacting signals, states, and transitions that can be modeled. My upcoming SULI at Oak Ridge working on multi-sensor inputs for scene-data fusion is a step towards the engineering mindset that will serve me well in designing and building future human-centered projects.

All of these experiences have inspired me to start my most recent project: an undergraduate research thesis investigating the feasibility of applying neuromorphic computing algorithms to analyze circulating tumor DNA biomarkers in the blood. If successful, my research could inform the production of rapid point-of-care liquid biopsy devices for non-invasive cancer monitering.

My long term goal is to establish the interdisciplinary skillset needed for true innovation in the 21st century. With all of these tools in mind, I aim to strategically position myself in the radically changing technological landscape by directly advancing human health on Earth and hopefully, beyond.

If you're interested in my work (present or future direction), please reach out and connect!

2025.05 — 2025.07 University of Mississippi Oxford, MS — NSF REU
biophysics DLS unsupervised ML SEM fluorometry

Hg²⁺-induced DNA nanoparticle formation and binding dynamics completed

Investigated how mercury ions drive nanoparticle assembly with short DNA fragments — characterizing binding dynamics and nanoparticle morphology for biomaterials and biosensing applications.

  • Ran 500+ DLS assays on mercury–DNA samples
  • Independently built an unsupervised ML pipeline to analyze noisy, complex DLS signals — revealing size distributions too wide for classical DLS fitting

  • Designed spectrofluorometric assays that resolved a gap in the literature: determined a 3:1 Hg:DNA binding ratio, a 14.4 µM Kd, and a 2.81 cooperativity constant
  • Delivered a 15-minute final talk to 30+ faculty and students
